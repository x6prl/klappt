Formula Knobs

These are the main formula-shape controls. They correspond to the engine’s seed, reward headroom, recovery, and due-mapping stages.

How success headroom is computed from predicted retrievability: baseline uses `1-r`, floor clamps it upward, additive adds a bonus, hybrid does both.
How the first successful review turns quality into initial stability: `linear` interpolates directly, `expo` expands high-quality seeds much more aggressively.
How stability becomes the next due interval: `linear` uses the raw half-life mapping, `power` bends the curve by exponent around the pivot day.
Exponent applied to success headroom. Lower than `1` makes near-due successes matter more; higher than `1` makes them flatter.
Minimum success headroom before exponentiation. This prevents successful reviews from flattening too early when predicted recall is already high.
Extra additive reward headroom on successful reviews. Useful when you want success to keep pushing intervals even at high predicted recall.
Multiplier bonus on successful reviews after recent failures. This is what makes “wrong several times, then right” recover faster.
How many recent failures can contribute to the recovery boost. Larger windows remember failure streaks longer.
Lower bound of the first-review quality scale. This controls how harsh the very first bad review can be.
Upper bound of the first-review quality scale. This is the main driver of how big the first perfect interval can get.
Shape parameter for seed scaling. Higher values make top-end seed growth kick in later; lower values make it kick in earlier.
Exponent used by the power due mapping. Below `1` compresses very large intervals and fattens early growth; above `1` does the reverse.
Reference day for the power due mapping. Around this point the powered curve behaves like the base curve.
